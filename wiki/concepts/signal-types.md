# Signal Types (Scoring Dimensions)

## Definition

The five dimensions along which every lead is evaluated by the Scoring Agent. Each dimension maps to a specific category of evidence in the enriched lead record. Together they produce sub-scores that sum to the overall 0–100 lead score.

These are not just analytical categories — they are the structural skeleton of the scoring prompt. The Prompt Layer encodes each dimension as a named section in the system message with an explicit definition of what "high" and "low" look like for that tenant.

## The Five Dimensions

### 1. Fit — Does this lead match our ICP?

**Default weight: 25%**

Evidence type: who the lead *is*, from enrichment.
- Company industry, size, geography — do they match the ICP target segment?
- Role seniority — is this the decision-maker or influencer the tenant targets?
- Wrong industry or role → low fit, regardless of intent signals.

Example lead fields: job title, company size, industry, location, LinkedIn profile.

---

### 2. Intent — How strong is their purchase interest?

**Default weight: 25%**

Evidence type: what the lead *said or did* that signals buying intent.
- Specific pricing / timeline / scope questions → high intent.
- Generic "tell me more" questions → low intent.
- Urgency language ("Q2", "ASAP", "this week") → high.
- Budget or authority signals ("50-seat team", "procurement approval") → high.
- Message progression matters: the arc from generic → integration-specific → pricing is itself an intent signal.

Example lead fields: message history, keywords detected, urgency indicators, budget/authority indicators.

---

### 3. Engagement — How active are they across channels and time?

**Default weight: 20%**

Evidence type: how much the lead has *interacted*, quantitatively.
- High frequency of touchpoints → high.
- Multi-channel presence → boost.
- Single one-and-done interaction → low.
- Recency modifies engagement: <24h = full points; 1–7 days = partial; >30 days = decays.

**Note:** Recency is not a separate sixth dimension — it is a modifier applied inside the Engagement dimension.

Example lead fields: total touchpoints, channels used, frequency, recency of last interaction, channel diversity.

---

### 4. Behaviour — What patterns do their actions reveal?

**Default weight: 20%**

Evidence type: actions the lead has taken that are harder to fake than words.
- Pricing page views, case study views → strong.
- Full form completion → strong.
- Asset downloads (whitepapers, pricing PDFs) → strong.
- Email opens and clicks → moderate signal.
- Short visits with no interaction → weak.

Behavioural signals are often richer than intent signals because actions reveal commitment without requiring the lead to articulate it.

Example lead fields: page views (by page type), downloaded assets, form completion depth, email open/click rates, time on site.

---

### 5. Context — What circumstances surround this lead?

**Default weight: 10%**

Evidence type: where and how the lead arrived.
- Source quality: enterprise webinar > targeted cold form > random ad.
- Campaign match: targeted campaign = boost.
- Geography match with ICP → confirmation signal.
- Time of day, business hours.
- UTM campaign data.

Context provides prior probability that this lead is serious, before the system evaluates their specific behaviour.

Example lead fields: lead source, first touch / referring URL, UTM campaign, device, time of day, geography match flag.

---

## How Weights Work

Default weights apply to all tenants unless overridden by the PersonaObject. A tenant can configure their own weights through the persona (see [[concepts/persona-layer]]). The Prompt Layer encodes the active weights in the system message so the LLM scores each dimension independently, then combines them.

**Example override:** "If a client says 'you're rating engagement too high', lower the engagement weight in their persona from 20 to 10. Same LLM, same pipeline, different behaviour — because the persona changed."

## Connection to the Data Model

Each dimension corresponds to specific fields in the enriched lead record (EnrichedLead) and their stored counterparts in the data entity model:

| Dimension | Stored as | Entity |
|---|---|---|
| All five | Signal questions | `signal` (generated by Signal Agent per tenant) |
| All five | Per-lead answers | `signal_evaluation` (filled in at Lead Enrichment stage) |
| Score outcome | Dimension scores + total | `lead_score` |

The `signal` and `signal_evaluation` entities (see [[concepts/data-entity-model]]) are the data model backing the fill-in-the-blanks prompt mechanism. Signal questions are generated by the Signal Agent during Pipeline 2 (Onboarding). Signal evaluations are filled deterministically at the Lead Enrichment stage in Pipeline 1.

## Critical Coupling: Enrichment → Prompt

The signals the LLM can evaluate are strictly bounded by what enrichment produces. If enrichment doesn't capture a field, the LLM cannot use it — regardless of how well the prompt is written.

> "The design of the enrichment layer and the design of the prompt are tightly coupled. You can't design the prompt in isolation — you have to know what the EnrichedLead will contain."

This means: adding a new scoring signal (e.g. LinkedIn connection depth) requires enrichment to first capture it, then the prompt template to reference it, then the Signal Agent to generate a signal question for it.

## Why It Matters

The five-dimension structure is what makes scores **auditable**. The LLM doesn't return a single number — it returns a breakdown (fit=26, intent=27, engagement=18, recency=15). When a sales rep disputes a score, the sub-score breakdown shows exactly which dimension drove the result, enabling targeted correction (wrong enrichment → fix enrichment; wrong weight → adjust persona).

## Tensions & Contradictions

None. Dimension names and default weights are locked in the design spec. Per-tenant overrides via persona are by design.
